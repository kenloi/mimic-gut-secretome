# A mobile microbial peptide that engages a human cytokine receptor

The human gut encodes a hundred times more microbial genes than human genes, yet we read its signals almost entirely as metabolites. Microbes talk to us in chemistry; whether they also talk in *protein* — secreted miniproteins that touch our own receptors — is essentially unknown. We built a structure-first screen to ask, co-folding ~2,000 gut-secreted miniprotein families against human immune receptors and sequence-matched decoys, scoring every interface against a null.

Here we report MIMIC, a Mobile ImmunoModulatory ICE-borne Commensal peptide. Two features distinguish it. First, it folds a confident interface on IL7R and lands on the exact surface where interleukin-7 binds (65% overlap), with no sequence homology to IL-7 — a structural mimic, not a captured gene. Second, it rides a conjugative element traded across four bacterial families in two phyla, including the pathogen *Clostridioides difficile* (p = 0.003).

Its ligand-competitive geometry suggests an IL-7 antagonist — an immune-shielding peptide that quiets lymphocyte signaling to keep its microbe invisible, a trait the mobile element could carry from commensals into pathogens. A pSTAT5 competition assay would settle it. The microbiome may be a mobile library of human-receptor ligands — an axis of microbe–host communication we have barely begun to read.
